Amazon's 16 Leadership Principles — Explained

A practical breakdown of the principles that guide how Amazon hires, evaluates performance, and makes decisions — with real-world examples for interviews and day-to-day work.

Overview Who this is for All 16 principles Key learnings Resources

Overview

Amazon's Leadership Principles are not motivational posters — they are the actual framework used in every hiring decision, performance review, and product discussion at Amazon. Understanding them is essential if you are preparing for an Amazon interview, and genuinely useful for any professional who wants to think more clearly about their work.

Each principle below includes the core idea, what it looks like in practice, and an example of how you might demonstrate it in a behavioural interview using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result).

Who this is for

Preparing for Amazon interviews Cloud / engineering professionals Anyone interested in tech leadership No technical background required

All 16 principles

01

Customer Obsession

Leaders start with the customer and work backwards. They work vigorously to earn and keep customer trust. Although leaders pay attention to competitors, they obsess over customers.

Example: Before building a feature, interview 10 customers about their actual pain points rather than assuming you know the answer.
02

Ownership

Leaders act on behalf of the entire company, beyond just their own team. They never say "that's not my job." They think long term and don't sacrifice long-term value for short-term results.

Example: Spotting a bug in another team's code during a code review and fixing it or raising it immediately rather than ignoring it.
03

Invent and Simplify

Leaders expect and require innovation from their teams and always find ways to simplify. They are externally aware, look for new ideas from everywhere, and are not limited by "not invented here."

Example: Replacing a 15-step manual deployment process with a single GitHub Actions workflow that runs in under 3 minutes.
04

Are Right, A Lot

Leaders have strong judgment and good instincts. They seek diverse perspectives and work to disconfirm their beliefs. Being right means being willing to change your mind when the data changes.

Example: Recommending a technical approach, but then pivoting after a junior team member raises a valid scalability concern with evidence.
05

Learn and Be Curious

Leaders are never done learning and always seek to improve themselves. They are curious about new possibilities and act to explore them. Curiosity is the foundation of innovation.

Example: Spending a weekend following an AWS hands-on lab not for a certification, but simply because you want to understand how Lambda functions work.
06

Hire and Develop the Best

Leaders raise the performance bar with every hire and promotion. They recognise exceptional talent and move them throughout the organisation. They coach and mentor but are not afraid to hold the bar high.

Example: Refusing to make a hiring decision under time pressure when the best candidate has not yet been found.
07

Insist on the Highest Standards

Leaders have relentlessly high standards. They continually raise the bar and drive their teams to deliver high-quality products, services, and processes. Defects are not passed down the line.

Example: Sending a document back for revision because the analysis is incomplete, even when the deadline is close.
08

Think Big

Thinking small is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Leaders create and communicate a bold direction that inspires results. They think differently and look around corners for ways to serve customers.

Example: Proposing to migrate a legacy on-premises system to the cloud rather than just patching it for another year.
09

Bias for Action

Speed matters in business. Many decisions and actions are reversible and do not need extensive study. Leaders value calculated risk-taking and move fast when the cost of delay exceeds the cost of being wrong.

Example: Deploying a quick fix to production (with a rollback plan) rather than waiting two weeks for the perfect solution.
10

Frugality

Accomplish more with less. Constraints breed resourcefulness, self-sufficiency, and invention. There are no extra points for headcount, budget size, or fixed expense.

Example: Architecting a solution using S3 + Lambda instead of a dedicated server, reducing costs by 80% with better reliability.
11

Earn Trust

Leaders listen attentively, speak candidly, and treat others respectfully. They are vocally self-critical, even when it is awkward, and do not believe their own or their team's body odour smells of perfume.

Example: Publicly acknowledging a mistake in a project post-mortem and sharing what you will do differently next time.
12

Dive Deep

Leaders operate at all levels, stay connected to the details, and audit frequently. No task is beneath them. When the data and anecdotes differ, they are sceptical and investigate.

Example: Tracing a performance problem all the way from a user complaint to a specific slow database query rather than dismissing it as "intermittent."
13

Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit

Leaders are obligated to respectfully challenge decisions when they disagree, even if doing so is uncomfortable. Once a decision is made, they commit wholly and do not undermine it.

Example: Pushing back in a design review with evidence, losing the argument gracefully, then delivering the agreed solution with full effort.
14

Deliver Results

Leaders focus on the key inputs for their business and deliver them with the right quality and in a timely fashion. Despite setbacks, they rise to the occasion and never settle.

Example: Completing a project on time despite losing a team member mid-sprint by redistributing the work and cutting scope intelligently.
15

Strive to Be Earth's Best Employer

Leaders work every day to create a safer, more productive, higher-performing, more diverse, and more just work environment. They lead with empathy and have fun at work.

Example: Proactively creating onboarding documentation so new team members can ramp up independently without feeling overwhelmed.
16

Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility

Amazon started in a garage and is now a large company. Leaders acknowledge that their actions have an outsized impact on the world and commit to being better every day.

Example: Choosing a cloud architecture that minimises unnecessary resource consumption rather than defaulting to over-provisioned infrastructure.

Key learnings

In behavioural interviews, use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to structure stories that demonstrate each principle.
The principles are not mutually exclusive — the best stories demonstrate two or three at once (e.g. Ownership + Deliver Results + Dive Deep).
"Disagree and Commit" is one of the most misunderstood principles. The disagreement must happen loudly and before the decision — silent non-compliance afterwards is not the intention.
Prepare at least one specific story per principle. Vague, generic answers are the most common reason candidates fail Amazon interviews.
